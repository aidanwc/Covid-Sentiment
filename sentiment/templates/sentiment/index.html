    About This Project

    Why We Made This

    Hi there,

    Our names are Julian and Aidan. Like many others in the past few months, we had a lot of questions about COVID-19 and some free time to try and answer them. More specifically, we wanted to analyze tweets that mention COVID-19 to gain an understanding of how humans behave during a situation as atypical as a pandemic.

    So, we built Covidsentiment.ca to do just that.

    How It Works

    Our program uses Twitter data to try and estimate how people are feeling about the virus.

    To do so, we gather nearly 50,000 tweets that mention COVID-19 every day, and perform sentiment analysis — which is a subfield of natural language processing, or ‘NLP’ — on them to determine to which degree each tweet is positive, negative, or neutral. The result is a score that ranges from -1.0 (negative sentiment) to 1.0 (positive sentiment), which we calculate by averaging all of the scores from the tweets that we scrape on a given day.

    Then, our program takes that number and plots it on a time series graph so we can easily see how the average sentiment is changing over time.

    In simpler terms, we use a computer program to gather thousands of tweets every day, analyze each tweet to determine its mood, and then track how the average mood of all the tweets changes over time.

    Research Questions

    1. What insights can we learn about the state of the pandemic by estimating what people’s moods are?

    2. How will the average mood towards COVID-19 change over time? What are the implications of that change?

    3. What important information can we learn from analyzing extremely negative tweets? What about extremely positive tweets?

    4. Are mood improvements permanent? Are they cyclical?

    5. What can we predict in the real-world from aggregate mood changes?

    Limitations

    As with any study, there are limitations. Two issues that may limit our study are representativeness and sample bias, which we discuss below.

    Despite these limitations, we believe that gathering data from a sufficiently large sample of users still offers interesting insights that can help answer some of our research questions.

    Representativeness

    Are Twitter users really representative of the general population? According to Pew Research, Twitter users are younger, more likely to lean left politically, more highly educated, and have higher incomes than U.S. adults overall.

    Additionally, not everyone has access to the internet, even in developed countries. And perhaps most importantly, the act of posting to Twitter is a decision that is likely impacted by mood, especially with an issue that is deeply intertwined with emotion such as COVID-19.

    This could further skew the results of our analysis if users tend to post more tweets when they’re in good moods, for example.

    Sample Bias

    Which tweets are we gathering, exactly?

    Twitter’s free streaming API returns only a small fraction (1%) of the total tweets on the entire social platform at a given time. The way that Twitter samples these tweets is also unknown, and there is evidence that these tweets can be biased.

    Despite these limitations, the ease-of-access and sheer volume of social media data provide a useful opportunity to analyze large amounts of data on human behaviour.
